# Council decision 0001 — How does Day14 get customer #1?

**Date:** 2026-05-15
**Decision class:** Customer acquisition strategy, ~$1k–$5k revenue impact
**Asked by:** Jack
**Status:** Logged. Action item assigned.

---

## The question

In the next 14 days, what's the highest-leverage move to land Day14's
first paying non-Jack customer? Options on the table:

- **A.** Cold DM SWFL service businesses on Instagram (current vision doc plan)
- **B.** Build a free customer-zero for a friend's business as a public case study
- **C.** Paid local Meta/Google ads ($300 test)
- **D.** In-person walk-ins at SWFL strip malls
- **E.** Niche announce in pool-service trade Facebook groups, leaning on Splash Jacks

---

## Stage 1 — The five advisors

### Advisor 1 — The Contrarian

All five options fail in the same way. Cold DM = generic pitch in a
saturated inbox; the owner's nephew already offered to "do their
website." Free-for-a-friend = friend never gives the brutal feedback a
paying customer would; you end up with a portfolio piece nobody trusts.
Paid ads at $300 buys you ~15 clicks in this niche — statistically zero
signal. Walk-ins burn Jack's only finite resource (time) on the highest-
CAC channel. Trade FB groups will ban you for self-promo within 24h. The
real failure mode isn't picking the wrong channel — it's debating the
five options for two weeks and shipping none of them. Customer #1
doesn't come from a strategy. It comes from finding someone who's
pissed-off enough about their current vendor TODAY that they'd switch
on a phone call. None of these channels find that person.

### Advisor 2 — The Reframer

The question is wrong. "How do I get customer #1" assumes the next move
is acquisition. The actual question is: "What's the cheapest test that
tells me Day14 should exist as a business at all?" Right now Day14 is a
marketing site + an offer. The market hasn't voted. The real first step
isn't sell — it's float. Record a 5-minute Loom: you fork the template,
run the brand-swap, push a preview URL. Post it three places (one pool
group, one SWFL business group, one founder Discord). Watch what
happens. Zero replies = the offer is wrong, not the channel. Three+
DMs = channel is fine and you know which vertical replied first. That's
$0 and two hours of work and tells you 10x more than any acquisition
strategy.

### Advisor 3 — The Expansionist

Customer #1 is the wrong target. Customer #1 is one person. You want a
wave. Splash Jacks Pools is already a live production app — make THAT
the case study right now. Record a 5-min walkthrough. Tweet it, post to
Hacker News Show HN, post to r/Entrepreneur, drop it in 3 founder
Discords. Position: "I rebuilt my pool service company's stack as a
productized agency. I'll do yours in 14 days for $2.5k flat." That
post can do what cold DM can't — generate 5-10 inbound leads in the
same week. You don't get customer #1, you get customers #1 through #5
simultaneously. Pick the best fit, decline the rest with a waitlist
(creates demand and gives you pricing power).

### Advisor 4 — The Outsider

Wait — Jack already has a customer. Splash Jacks Pools is Jack's pool
company. That counts. The whole framing of "first customer" sounds like
founder LARP. You're the founder, you're the first customer, your own
production app is your case study, your testimonial is yourself. The
real question is: what's the first non-Jack customer? Probably someone
in Jack's existing network who already trusts him before he says a
word. Look at the last 30 days of his texts and DMs — who runs a
service business and has complained about their website or their
booking? That person is customer #1. Cold acquisition is for when
warm is exhausted. It isn't.

### Advisor 5 — The Executor

Monday morning. Pick five SWFL service businesses you've personally
seen — the pool guy, the lawn guy, the mobile detail truck, the AC
repair van, the junk hauling truck. Screenshot their websites tonight
(probably trash). DM each owner directly on Instagram tomorrow:
*"Saw your truck on Pine Island Rd. Your website's costing you jobs —
I built mine in 14 days for $2,500 flat, can do yours next week. Want
a preview before you decide?"* Five DMs Sunday night. By Wednesday you
know if anyone bites. No strategy needed. Five messages.

(Mapping shuffled.)

**Response A** — Five DMs to specific people Jack has physically seen.
**Response B** — Splash Jacks is already a customer; mine Jack's warm network.
**Response C** — Public case study video, post broadly, get 5-10 inbound at once.

### Reviewer notes

**Strongest:** Response C. It's the only one that converts Jack's
existing asset (Splash Jacks as a live production case study) into
demand, instead of generating demand from scratch. The other four all
assume Day14 has no proof; Response C says "you already have proof,
use it." Highest expected return per hour of work.

**Biggest blind spot:** Response A. The five-DMs move is great as a
tactic but the sample size is too small to tell you anything generalizable.
If zero bite, Jack won't know whether the offer is wrong, the
channel is wrong, or just five wasn't enough.

**What all five missed:** Pricing as the acquisition lever. $2,500 may
be too high (most SWFL service businesses currently pay $0 for a website
their nephew built) or too low (signals "cheap" to better customers).
Acquisition channel matters less than whether the OFFER prices itself
into a yes. None of the five advisors poked at the price.

## Stage 3 — The Chairman

### Recommendation

This week, run two cheap parallel tests; let the market vote by Friday.

1. **By Sunday night:** Record a 5-minute Splash Jacks walkthrough video
   on Loom or QuickTime. Show the admin panel, the customer portal, and
   say *"I made my pool company's stack a productized agency. I'll do
   yours in 14 days for $2,500 flat."* Post Monday morning to: one
   pool-service Facebook group, one SWFL business Facebook group, one
   founder Discord, and your own Instagram.

2. **By Tuesday:** Send 5 DMs from the Executor's list — specific
   trucks/vans you've personally seen in Cape Coral / Fort Myers /
   Bonita. Personalized opener referencing where you saw them.

3. **By Friday:** Whichever channel produced more signal (DMs to your
   inbox, comment threads, reply rate), double down for week 2. The
   other channel, drop. Don't run both at full intensity for more than
   a week — split the difference and learn fast.

### The single most important reason

You don't know yet which channel actually works in SWFL for this offer.
Channels lie until tested. Two cheap tests in parallel cost you ~4
hours total and give you a real signal by Friday. Two weeks of
strategy-picking gives you zero signal.

### Concrete next step today (Friday May 15)

Open QuickTime. Record a rough 5-minute Splash Jacks walkthrough — no
script, no polish, just narrate what you built and why. Save to:

  `~/Documents/studio/marketing/case-study-video-v1.mov`

Don't edit it. Don't worry about the lighting. The video being
unpolished is part of the proof — operator, not agency.

## Notes for future Council runs

- Format works. 5 advisors + anonymized review + Chairman keeps the
  decision moving without devolving into pros/cons paralysis.
- Time to run: ~25 min if you write each advisor at 100-150 words.
- The "what all five missed" question is the highest-value part of the
  review — it's the one most likely to surface a non-obvious frame.
- Log every Council run here as `NNNN-short-name.md` in numeric order.
  Quarterly: re-read the log, see which Chairman calls aged well.
